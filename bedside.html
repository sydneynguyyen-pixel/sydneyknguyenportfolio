Context

AI for Social Good · Hack with MLH & DigitalOcean · 2nd Place Overall

"How might we bring the same care to caregivers that they bring to patients?"

Support the people at the bedside, not just the patient in the bed.

In home hospice care, the most important information about a patient often lives in a single handwritten notebook passed between shifts. It can be lost, hard to read, and — worst of all — it can't tell anyone when something urgent has changed.

The most critical information in home hospice lived in a notebook anyone could lose.

Care passes between shifts through a paper binder at the bedside. Notes get illegible, pages go missing, and a change that needs a nurse's attention sits silently until the next person happens to read it. There was no way to flag urgency, and no way to catch up quickly on what happened while you were away.

The people spending the most time at the bedside had the least support.

Federal rules require volunteers to provide at least 5% of all hospice care hours (42 CFR 418.78) — yet they receive no specialized tools despite carrying real care responsibilities. We built for that structurally overlooked user first: someone who needs to log a note, ask a question, and hand off cleanly, without a login screen standing in the way.

Bedside is a mobile web app that replaces the paper binder — letting anyone at the bedside capture notes by voice, get answers grounded in the patient's actual care plan, and hand off to the next shift in seconds, all while keeping sensitive medical detail visible only to the people who should see it.

My Role

UX/UI Design Role-Based Interface Voice Interaction React Build Figma Prototyping

Our team was just two people. Michelle built and deployed the backend — the AI agents, Supabase infrastructure, and escalation logic that turn a spoken note into a structured, flagged handoff. I owned the design and the frontend: I designed the role-based interface in Figma, built it out in React, and implemented the voice capture that lets volunteers log notes just by speaking. In a hackathon with a team this small, the goal was to make something genuinely usable at a real bedside feel effortless — so most of my decisions came back to removing friction, not adding features.

Design principles

01
Zero friction to start. No app store, no accounts. Scan a QR code, pick your role, enter a PIN — you're in.
02
Speak, don't type. Notes get captured in the moment, by voice, so caregiving hands stay free and nothing gets lost to "I'll write it down later."
03
Show only what each role needs. Volunteers see comfort and care; nurses see full medical detail. The interface enforces the boundary so no one has to.
04
Privacy is the baseline, not a setting. All the trust of the bedside binder, none of the ways it can go wrong.

Process

Design and development happened at the same time — Michelle wiring up agents while I moved from Figma into React, sharing one source of truth as we went. I started by mapping the one moment that mattered most: the handoff. Everything a caregiver does in an app like this is in service of the next person picking up cleanly, so I designed backward from that — what does the arriving shift need to see first, and how do we get the leaving shift to leave it behind without extra work?

Because the app would be used one-handed at a bedside, I designed it mobile-only from the first frame. Access had to be the lightest part of the whole experience, so I built the flow around a QR scan, a role choice, and a short PIN — no onboarding, no password reset, nothing to forget. From there the two roles branch: a volunteer's view stays focused on comfort and care, while a nurse's opens up to full medical detail. Designing that split in Figma first gave the React build a clear target instead of a moving one.

Final Product

Bedside turns a spoken note into a structured, urgency-flagged handoff — in one continuous motion, with nobody choosing a priority or filling out a form.

Scan-and-go access

A QR scan, a role, and a PIN. No app store download and no accounts, so anyone at the bedside can start in seconds.

Voice-to-structured logging

Volunteers speak their notes; the AI organizes them into clean entries and auto-flags how urgent each one is.

Care-plan Q&A

A grounded assistant that answers questions only from the patient's actual care plan, and refuses to guess or give clinical advice it wasn't given.

Effortless handoffs

AI-generated catch-ups summarize everything that's changed since the last visit, so the arriving shift is oriented immediately.

Role-based views

Volunteers see comfort and care information; nurses get full medical detail — the interface enforces the line automatically.

Automatic nurse escalation

Urgent notes route to a nurse on their own, so a critical change never waits for someone to notice it.

Challenges

The hardest design problem was serving two very different roles without building two different apps — a volunteer's comfort-focused view and a nurse's full medical view had to feel like one coherent product, not a settings toggle. On the build side, we hit a wall of technical hurdles in a compressed window: our inference calls kept returning 403 errors until we switched models, an instruction to output "valid JSON only" was quietly suppressing the agent's tool calls until Michelle restructured how those tools were described, and our frontend and knowledge base briefly described two different patients until we committed to a single source of truth and re-indexed. In a two-person hackathon, every one of those was a shared fire drill — and fixing them together is a big part of why the end-to-end flow actually held together by the deadline.

Built with — React · Vite · Tailwind · Web Speech API (on-device dictation) · Supabase (Postgres + Edge Functions) · RAG agents on DigitalOcean Gradient / GLM 5.2 · function calling via the OpenAI SDK. Three agents did the heavy lifting: a Handoff Summarizer (voice → structured JSON with urgency flags + nurse escalation), a Care-Plan Q&A RAG agent, and a Pattern Analyst that reads trends across recent shift logs.

The best tool for a caregiver is the one they don't have to think about.

Every instinct in a hackathon pushes you toward more features. Bedside kept teaching me the opposite: the win was everything we removed. No account, no download, no priority dropdown, no form. When the person at the bedside can scan, speak, and hand off without breaking their attention from the patient, the technology has done its job by disappearing.

Designing for social good meant designing for the person everyone else forgot.

The volunteers who provide a mandated share of hospice hours had no tools built for them at all. Starting from that overlooked user — instead of the nurse or the software — changed what "good design" meant here: not the most capable interface, but the most trustworthy and the lightest. That reframing shaped every screen.

Given more time: piloting Bedside with a real home-hospice team · adding offline support for spotty bedside connectivity · pressure-testing accessibility for older volunteers · expanding the pattern analysis that spots trends across recent shifts.

Thank you to my teammate Michelle Bui for building the entire backend and every AI agent behind this in one weekend — the best kind of two-person team, and somehow we took 2nd place overall.

Thank you to Major League Hacking and DigitalOcean for hosting AI for Social Good, and for pointing a hackathon at problems that actually matter.
